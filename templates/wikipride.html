LGBTQIA+ flags

×

The first pride flag created by artist Gilbert Baker in 1978. A rainbow was chosen as it is recognised as a symbol of hope.
Each colour has a different meaning:
Pink: Sex
Red: Life
Orange: Healing
Yellow: Sunlight
Green: Nature
Turquoise: Magic
Blue: Harmony
Violet: Spirit

The first pride flag
×

The pink and turquoise colours were dropped to make the flag easier to mass produce. It still stands as a symbol of love and pride, and is often used by businesses to show their support.

The current international pride flag
×

This flag was updated by Daniel Quasar to include the colours of the trans flag (pink, white, blue), and the black and brown colours give representation to the marginalised people of the community. The arrow shape shows that progress needs to be made, but we are moving in the right direction.

The progress flag
×

This flag was created by Michael Page to have a symbol for bisexual people to feel connected to.
Pink: same-sex attraction
Blue: Opposite sex attraction
Lavender: Attraction to both sexes

The bisexual flag
×

This flag was created by Monica Helms, a transgender woman in 1999.
Blue represents boys.
Pink represents girls.
White represents those who are transitioning, have no gender or are gender neutral.

The transgender flag
×

This flag was created by Kye Rowan for the people who weren’t represented by the current flags.
Yellow represents genders outside of gender binary
White represents people who identify with more than one gender
Purple represents genders that are a mix of male and female
Black represents people who are agender

The non-binary flag
×

This flag was created in 2010 to raise awareness of asexuality in the community.
Black: represents asexuality (no sexual attraction)
Grey: represents grey asexuality, where some sexual attraction is felt by individuals
White: represents sexuality
Purple: represents community

The asexual flag
×

Unsure who created it, but it has gathered favour and attention over the last 10 years as a symbol of attraction to all genders.
Blue: attraction to males
Pink: attraction to females
Yellow: attraction to non-binary people

The pansexual flag
×

This flag was created by JJ Poole to represent the people whose gender identity shifts.
Pink represents feminity
White represents all genders
Purple represents masculinity and femininity
Black represents a lack of gender
Blue represents masculinity

The genderfluid flag
×

This flag was created by Morgan Carpenter to represent intersex people and break down the incorrect stereotypes.
The gold/yellow was chosen after being inspired by a story they were told to reclaim the hermaphrodite slur.
The purple circle represents a community which is unbroken; whole and complete.

The intersex flag
×

The black and white represents the heterosexual cisgender people
The rainbow colours represent the LGBTQIA+ community, in the shape of an ‘A’ to represent allies.
